Immigration to Australia

Exploring the trend in the origin of immigrants coming to Australia from 1980 to 2022

Immigration has significantly shaped Australia’s demographic and economic growth. Post-World War II, the country encouraged large-scale European migration to build its workforce. The end of the White Australia Policy in the 1970s led to a more diverse influx, especially from Asia. Economic opportunities and political stability continue to attract skilled workers, students, and refugees.

Immigration trends have fluctuated, with slower rates during global crises like the COVID-19 pandemic and growth during periods of labor shortages.

There is an overall upwards trend in immigration, with the numbers steadily increasing from around 150,000 in 1980 to a peak of nearly 600,000 before 2020. A significant drop occurs in 2019, coinciding with the global COVID-19 pandemic, which drastically affected migration flows due to travel restrictions. However, by 2022, the number of immigrants rebounds strongly, exceeding pre-pandemic levels.

Asia has been the dominant source of immigrants to Australia, particularly after the 2000s, as seen by the dark green shades representing high immigration levels. Europe, once a major contributor, shows a decline in immigration over the years, while Africa and Oceania maintain moderate but steady trends. North America shows a slight increase in recent decades, and South America remains the least significant contributor, though it has experienced a gradual rise.

The pandemic's impact is also evident, with immigration levels falling sharply in 2020 across all continents before recovering by 2022.

Economic downturns in their own countries, prompted people to seek better opportunities abroad. Australia's strong economy, political stability, and high quality of life were attractive. The Trans-Tasman Travel Arrangement allowed easy movement for New Zealanders, while strong historical, cultural, and linguistic ties between the UK and Australia facilitated immigration from the UK.

Today, major source countries include India, China, and the Philippines, reflecting Australia’s skill-based immigration system and humanitarian commitments.
